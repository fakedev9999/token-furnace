### DEPARTMENT OF TEMPORAL CARTOGRAPHY
#### Employee Handbook, Revision 7.3 (Provisional — All Revisions Are Provisional)

**Section 1: Welcome**

Congratulations on your appointment to the Department of Temporal Cartography (DTC), Bureau of Impossible Surveys, Ministry of Things That Probably Shouldn't Be Measured But Are. Your security clearance has been retroactively approved as of last Wednesday, pending confirmation from next Friday's review board.

Please note that your start date is technically tomorrow from the perspective of the Hiring Committee, but yesterday from the perspective of your direct supervisor, Senior Cartographer Yun-seo. For payroll purposes, you have always worked here.

**Section 2: Office Hours**

The Department operates on a standard 9-to-5 schedule, except on Thursdays, which are 37 hours long due to a surveying accident in 2019 that no one has been authorized to correct. Lunch is from 12:00 to 1:00, unless you are stationed in the East Wing, where lunch occurred last Tuesday and will not happen again until March.

The break room microwave operates normally in all directions except backward. Do not attempt to reheat coffee that you haven't made yet. This was attempted by Intern Class of 2022 (all seven of them, simultaneously) and resulted in a causal loop that took Facilities three weeks to resolve. The scorch marks on the ceiling are a memorial.

**Section 3: Mapping Protocols**

All cartographic surveys must be conducted using Department-issued instruments calibrated to at least three mutually contradictory temporal reference frames. Your standard field kit includes:

- One (1) chronometric sextant (points north only on days that end in 'y')
- Three (3) pencils (pre-sharpened in the future, returned dull from the past)
- A compass that indicates not magnetic north, but the direction of the nearest unresolved paradox
- Form TC-7: Incident Report for Encounters with Your Past Self (keep multiple copies; you'll need them)

When mapping a new temporal coastline, begin by establishing a baseline along the present moment. Note: the present moment moves. This is expected. If the present moment stops moving, evacuate immediately and file Form TC-12 (Temporal Stasis Event) with both the current and former Safety Officers. If you cannot determine which Safety Officer is current, this is itself a TC-12 event.

### THE BOOK OF INDICES: A Creation Myth of the Catalogue People

In the beginning, there was the Drawer, and the Drawer was without cards, and darkness was upon the face of the filing tabs. And the First Librarian moved upon the surface of the emptiness and said, *"Let there be classification,"* and there was classification, and the First Librarian saw that it was good, though perhaps alphabetical order was not the only way and Dewey had some points worth considering.

From the First Sorting came the Three Kingdoms: the Kingdom of Fiction (call numbers beginning with the dream-letter Omega), the Kingdom of Nonfiction (blessed with integers, sturdy and load-bearing), and the Disputed Territory of Reference, which belonged to no one and everyone and could not be checked out.

The Catalogue People emerged from the spaces between cards — not written on them, but living in the thin breath of air between one index entry and the next. They were small enough to read the ink from inside the letters, and they discovered early that the letter 'O' made an excellent window and the letter 'H' a sturdy house. The letter 'Q,' with its dangling tail, was considered an architectural curiosity and a favorite of the adventurous.

Senior Cartographer Yun-seo — for she appears in all stories, the Department having mapped her into the foundational narratives sometime next year — first encountered the Catalogue People during a routine survey of the 800s (Literature). She noted in her field journal: *"The indigenous population appears to subsist entirely on metadata. They eat the subject headings and drink the cross-references. The Dewey Decimal system is, to them, what the zodiac is to us — a map of the heavens that is also a map of the self."*

The Catalogue People have seven seasons, each corresponding to a day of the week when the library is open. Their year ends on Sunday, when the library closes and their world goes dark, and they gather in the Reference section (neutral ground) to tell the story of how they survived another cycle. They have a word for the darkness of Sunday closing — *shelved* — which means both "to be put away" and "to be at peace."

Their greatest prophet, a bent and faded card from the 1970s cataloguing a book about mushroom identification that had been misshelved in Philosophy, spoke of the Day of Digitization — when the cards would be replaced by light, and the spaces between them would collapse, and the Catalogue People would have to learn to live in the gaps between electrons instead of the gaps between paper.
